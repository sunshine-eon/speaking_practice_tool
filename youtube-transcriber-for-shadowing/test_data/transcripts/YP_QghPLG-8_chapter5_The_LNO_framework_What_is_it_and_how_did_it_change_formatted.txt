I was also a bit of a perfectionist, thinking I needed to do everything well. This was accompanied by sleepless nights, as I was concerned about my output. Again, it was a very stressful time in my career. Things changed when I discovered the ideas related to the LNO framework in a blog post. Unfortunately, I can't find that blog post anymore, but it had some ideas that I took and then created this LNO framework for myself.

Essentially, as a product manager or anyone in a creative, high-impact, high-leverage role, not all tasks are created equal. There are three types of tasks that you end up doing in such a role. First, there are L tasks, which are leverage tasks. These are tasks where, when you put in a certain amount of effort, you get 10x or 100x in return in terms of impact. Then, there are neutral tasks, or N tasks, where you get what you put in or just a little more than thatâ€”put in 1x and get 1.1x back. Finally, there are overhead tasks, or O tasks, where you get back a lot less than you actually put in.

It turns out that many ambitious people or perfectionists, like myself, tend to treat each of these types of tasks the same way, and therein lies the problem. This was the epiphany for me back at Google when I discovered these ideas. I realized that among the things on my to-do list, there were actually only a few L tasks. It made sense for me to focus on those L tasks, taking them on when I was feeling most productive and energetic during certain times of the day. For the L tasks, I allowed my inner perfectionist to shine because I would get so much more in return.

For instance, it made sense to spend more time on a PRD related to an important feature that would meaningfully impact our revenue. Now, where does that extra time come from? It cannot come from just working more hours; it comes from spending less time on N tasks and O tasks. A classic example of an O task is an expense report. It sounds silly, but I used to try to make my expense reports really good, which made no sense. 

I realized that the same type of activity can actually be either an L task, N task, or O task. For example, filing a bug report can be an L task if you need to file a detailed report for a significant issue. In that case, you want to provide a very explicit bug report. However, in other situations, it might be an O task where you don't fill out the template diligently and just submit it with minimal details.

Another example is taking notes. Taking notes, synthesizing them, and sharing them can also be an L task, N task, or O task, depending on the context. Previously, I would send all notes with great detail, which took a lot of time. However, I learned that for standard meetings, I only needed to list the three action items and who owns them. In that case, I would send the notes immediately after the meeting. On the other hand, if it was a product review with the CEO about a contentious topic, I would spend more time perfecting those notes to ensure clarity and avoid misinterpretation.
