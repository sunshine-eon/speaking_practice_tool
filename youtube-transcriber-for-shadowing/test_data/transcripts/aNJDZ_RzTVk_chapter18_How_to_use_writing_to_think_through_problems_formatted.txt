Throughout the years at Ramp, I was often faced with a problem or question that I couldn't answer off the bat. I had to go back to first principles. The best way of doing that is to shut down your laptop, take out a piece of paper, write the question as simply as possible at the top of the paper, and just spend time thinking about how to answer that question. 

There were a ton of questions over time, particularly around scalability problems that few companies have successfully tackled. You have to start with your own thinking. How do we scale decision-making? How do we incentivize teams to work together? How do we do headcount planning? How do we allocate headcount in a fair way? How do we avoid politics as firsthand data goes away? How do we make decisions on doubling down versus pivoting? All these things are really tough. 

I found that while reading can be helpful, it doesn't necessarily make you think better. It makes you wiser, but the best way to increase your capacity to think is to actually do the thinking. Writing helps with this; if you're able to write things clearly, you're able to think through things clearly. It was also a way for me to effectively communicate, especially during COVID, where we largely grew up during a time when everything was written. 

Writing was a way for me to get content out there to increase my brand and Ramp's brand, which ultimately led us to hire better people over time. All these things kind of worked out, but it does require you to block out time and focus on how you think about problems rather than just trying to Google the answer. After you've thought through it, then go out and read, and you'll fine-tune your thinking and identify new questions to ask yourself afterward.
